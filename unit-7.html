Learning Focus

Moving Beyond Qualitative Guesses

This unit introduced formal methods for quantifying risk and supporting complex decisions, challenging an over-reliance on qualitative scoring. The focus shifted from subjective risk ratings to evidence-based probabilistic analysis.

Key Reading Synthesis

Critical Review of MCDM Methods

Asadabadi et al. (2019): This was a critical review of AHP/ANP, providing a necessary dose of scepticism. It highlighted the potential for ranking reversal and the subjectivity in pairwise comparisons.

Key Insight

This taught me to see MCDM methods as structured guides for thinking, not mathematical magic bullets. Their utility depends heavily on the expertise of the participants defining the criteria and the quality of the input data.

Historical Context & Enterprise Application

Metropolis (1987) & Fraser & Simkins (Ch. 22): The historical context of the Monte Carlo method was fascinating. It illuminated the core concept: using computational power to model complex systems with inherent uncertainty by running thousands of simulations.

Enterprise Context

Fraser & Simkins contextualised this within ERM, showing how Monte Carlo simulation can model financial and operational risks to produce probability distributions of outcomes, which is far more informative than a single-point estimate.

Critical Analysis and Application

Complementary Methodologies

The readings created a powerful contrast between different quantitative approaches:

AHP/ANP for Structured Decision-Making

Provides a structured way to handle multi-criteria decisions (e.g., "Which risk should we treat first?") by breaking down complex problems into hierarchical structures and using pairwise comparisons.

Monte Carlo for Uncertainty Modeling

Handles deep uncertainty in the inputs themselves (e.g., "What is the probable financial impact range of this data breach?") by simulating thousands of possible scenarios based on probability distributions.

Practical Integration

For our project, this meant recognising that these are complementary tools for different stages of the risk analysis process:

  • AHP: For prioritizing which risks to address first based on multiple criteria
  • Monte Carlo: For understanding the potential impact range of high-priority risks
  • Bayesian Networks: For modeling complex causal relationships between risks

Key Methodological Insights

From Point Estimates to Distributions

The most significant shift was moving from single-point estimates ("this will cost £50,000") to probability distributions ("there's a 90% chance it will cost between £40,000-£60,000").

Tool Selection Criteria

Different quantitative methods serve different purposes:

  • Simple scenarios: Basic probability calculations
  • Multi-criteria decisions: AHP/ANP
  • Complex uncertainty: Monte Carlo simulation
  • Causal relationships: Bayesian networks

Data Quality Importance

All quantitative methods depend on the quality of input data. Garbage in, garbage out remains a fundamental principle regardless of methodological sophistication.
